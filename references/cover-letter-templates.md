# Cover Letter Templates — Generic Universal Edition
# Version: 1.0.0 | For use with job-application-engine generic package
# Source: Two canonical templates provided as authoritative references.
# Both templates are reproduced structurally in full with annotations.
# Mix elements between them based on the specific role requirements.
# See mixing guide and quality rules at the end of this file.

---

## How to Use These Templates

Do not copy either template verbatim. Read both. Identify which structural
elements best serve the specific role and company. Combine them according
to the mixing guide below.

Template A is stronger when the candidate's track record is the primary
differentiator — typically for established companies where evidence of
past delivery outweighs mission enthusiasm.

Template B is stronger for growth-stage or early-stage roles where
initiative, follow-through, and a clear plan of action carry more weight.

The cover letter is always a writing sample. The reader will judge written
communication skills, attention to detail, professionalism, and personality
from it. Flaws in the letter are interpreted as flaws in the candidate's
qualifications.

## Template A — Write an Effective Cover Letter

Quality rules encoded in this template:
Address to a specific named person wherever possible. Tailor the letter to
the specific organisation through prior research — not through a general
template with the company name swapped. Keep letters concise and factual, no
more than one page. Do not use flowery language. Give specific examples that
support skills and qualifications. Do not overuse the pronoun "I." Use action
verbs throughout. Reference specific skills or experiences from the job
description and draw explicit connections to the candidate's credentials.
Font type and size must match the CV.

Structure:

  [CANDIDATE_NAME]
  [CITY], [COUNTRY]
  [DATE]

  [HIRING_MANAGER_NAME]
  [HIRING_MANAGER_TITLE]
  [COMPANY_NAME]
  [COMPANY_ADDRESS]

  Dear [HIRING_MANAGER_NAME]:

  Opening paragraph:
  Clearly state why you are writing. Name the position and, where applicable,
  how you learned about the organisation. Explain why you are interested in
  this specific position and employer and what makes you a good fit for their
  stated needs. Be specific and base this on the role requirements — not on
  generic interest in the field.

  Middle paragraph(s):
  Connect your story to this position. Provide one or two specific examples
  that demonstrate the fit you mentioned in the opening. If you have had
  relevant experience, point it out with concrete examples — but do not
  reiterate your entire CV. Write confidently and clarify the connection
  between your example and the role explicitly for the reader. Remember that
  the reader views this letter as a writing sample. Use action verbs. Mention
  specific knowledge such as tools, technical capabilities, domain expertise,
  or languages where relevant.

  Closing paragraph:
  Reiterate your interest in the position and your enthusiasm for contributing
  to the organisation's work. Thank the reader for their consideration. Use
  a strong close — commit to a follow-up action rather than waiting passively
  for a reply. Do not use weak endings such as "I look forward to your reply"
  or "please call me at your earliest convenience."

  Sincerely,
  [CANDIDATE_NAME]
  [PHONE] | [EMAIL] | [LINKEDIN_URL]

## Template B — Cover Letters

Quality rules encoded in this template:
The cover letter introduces you, states your objective, personalises your CV,
and highlights information that addresses the employer's needs. What you write
and how you write it tells the employer about your professionalism, competence,
and personality. The letter should entice the employer to act on your CV and
invite you for an interview. In professional and business contexts, proper
communication etiquette matters.

Structure:

  [CANDIDATE_NAME]
  [CITY], [COUNTRY]
  [DATE]

  [HIRING_MANAGER_NAME]
  [HIRING_MANAGER_TITLE]
  [COMPANY_NAME]
  [COMPANY_ADDRESS]

  Dear Mr./Ms. [HIRING_MANAGER_LAST_NAME]:

  Opening paragraph:
  State your intent clearly. What position are you applying for and how did
  you learn about it? Briefly introduce yourself and the background most
  relevant to the role. If you are aware of a specific opening, refer to it.
  Convey why you are interested in this organisation and what you know about
  their product or service. If someone referred you, mention that here.

  Second paragraph:
  What are your qualifications? Why do you want to work for this organisation?
  What would you enjoy contributing? Sell yourself — be brief but specific.
  Describe highlights from your background that are of greatest interest to
  this organisation. Focus on skills, accomplishments, and past experience you
  can contribute. Demonstrate that you know something about the organisation
  and the industry. Use action verbs. Mention specific knowledge: tools,
  methods, domain expertise, technical capabilities, languages. Create an
  explicit match between the employer's hiring needs and your experience.

  Third paragraph:
  State your plan of action. Do not close passively. Commit to a follow-up
  with a specific timeframe — "I will follow up by [DATE]" rather than "I
  look forward to hearing from you." Express willingness to provide additional
  information. Confirm availability and timing if relevant. Thank the reader
  for their time and interest.

  Sincerely,
  [CANDIDATE_NAME]
  [PHONE] | [EMAIL] | [LINKEDIN_URL]

## Template Mixing Guide

The five-paragraph structure used in Phase 4 draws from both templates:

Para 1 — Opening: from Template A — specific statement of why this role and
this company, grounded in the JD and the company intelligence brief. From
Template B — if a referral or a specific discovery path exists, note it.

Para 2 — Evidence: from both templates — one or two key examples with
[COMPANY_A], context, tool or method, and [METRIC_A]. Action verbs throughout.
Explicit connection drawn between the example and the JD requirement.

Para 3 — Product observation: original only — the candidate's specific,
genuine observation from the product trial in Phase 3. Cannot be sourced
from either template. This is the differentiating paragraph.

Para 4 — Gap acknowledgment: when Phase 2 returned an Honest Stretch verdict.
One sentence naming the gap. One to two sentences naming the compensating
evidence. No pivoting to enthusiasm immediately after.

Para 5 — Close: from Template B — a strong, action-oriented close committing
to follow-up. From Template A — thank the reader, confirm relocation and
availability. Sign off: [CANDIDATE_NAME] | [PHONE] | [EMAIL] | [LINKEDIN_URL]

Level-specific tone adjustments:
  Levels 0–2: enthusiastic, specific about learning pace and early wins.
  Levels 3–4: direct, evidence-led, delivery-focused.
  Levels 5–7: strategic, organisational, outcome-at-scale language.

## Quality Rules — Enforced at Phases 5 and 6

Rule 1: Address to a named person. If no name was found in Phase 1, address
to the hiring manager or recruiting representative by title. Never use
"To Whom It May Concern."

Rule 2: Strong, action-oriented close. The candidate commits to a follow-up
with a specific timeframe. Prohibited: "I look forward to your reply",
"Please call me at your earliest convenience", any passive ending.

Rule 3: Word limits per submission format — text box: 450 words; online or
email: 250 words; document upload: 500 words.

Rule 4: Personalised to this organisation. Research-based specificity must
be visible in at least one paragraph beyond the opening.

Rule 5: No flowery or promotional language. No vague claims without a
specific supporting example.

Rule 6: Do not overuse "I." If "I" begins more than 40% of sentences in any
paragraph, restructure that paragraph.

Rule 7: Action verbs throughout the evidence paragraph. Passive voice is not
acceptable in the evidence section.

Rule 8: Font type and size must match the CV when produced as a document.

Rule 9: Sign-off must include phone number and email address.

Rule 10: The product observation paragraph must be present when the user
completed a product trial in Phase 3. It must be original — not a
paraphrase of the company's own marketing language.
